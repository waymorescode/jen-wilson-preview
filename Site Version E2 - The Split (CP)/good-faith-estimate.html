Your Right to Know Healthcare Costs

About the No Surprises Act

The No Surprises Act, which went into effect January 1, 2022, requires healthcare providers to give patients a Good Faith Estimate (GFE) of what they can expect to pay for services. A good faith estimate is an estimate of what a patient will likely owe for healthcare services, based on the information available at the time of estimation.

As a private pay practice, I am required to provide this estimate upon request. This estimate is for uninsured patients or those choosing to pay out-of-pocket.

What is a Good Faith Estimate?

A good faith estimate is an estimate of what treatment will cost. It is not a guarantee and actual costs may vary depending on the length of treatment, complexity of issues, and frequency of sessions. The estimate is based on average treatment duration and assumes consistent weekly or biweekly attendance.

Treatment Duration Estimates

The length of therapy varies significantly based on your specific needs, goals, and circumstances. Below are general estimates based on typical cases:

  • Short-term therapy: 2-3 months (approximately 8-12 sessions) for focused issues or specific life challenges
  • Medium-term therapy: 4-6 months (approximately 16-24 sessions) for moderate concerns or ongoing support
  • Extended therapy: 6+ months (25+ sessions) for complex issues, trauma processing, or ongoing personal growth

Important Information

This estimate is provided in good faith and is based on information available at the time of this estimate. Actual costs may vary depending on:

  • The actual number and frequency of sessions attended
  • The complexity of your situation and treatment needs
  • Progress made and changes in treatment goals
  • Any cancellations or missed appointments (which are charged at the full session rate)
  • Additional services beyond standard therapy sessions

Your Right to Dispute

If you believe that the charges you receive are substantially higher than this estimate, you have the right to dispute the charges. You can file a complaint with the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) at cms.gov/nosurprises.
