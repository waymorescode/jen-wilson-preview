Couples Therapy

Rebuild Connection, Communication, and Trust

When Love Isn't Enough

You love each other, but you're not connecting anymore. The same arguments on repeat. The distance that's grown between you. The way you're roommates instead of partners. The loneliness of being in a relationship but feeling completely alone.

Maybe there's been betrayal—an affair, broken promises, lies that shattered trust. Maybe you're facing a major transition—new baby, job loss, illness—and it's pulling you apart instead of bringing you together. Maybe you're just exhausted from trying to fix things on your own.

Couples therapy isn't about assigning blame or deciding who's right. It's about understanding each other again, breaking destructive patterns, and rebuilding the connection that brought you together in the first place.

I Help Couples With

Communication Breakdowns

When every conversation turns into a fight. When you can't talk about anything important without shutting down or exploding. When you feel unheard, dismissed, or attacked. I'll teach you how to communicate in ways that actually work—how to express needs, listen deeply, and resolve conflicts without damaging each other.

Rebuilding Trust After Betrayal

Affairs, lies, broken promises—betrayal shatters the foundation of a relationship. The betrayed partner feels devastated, the other feels defensive or ashamed. Can trust be rebuilt? Sometimes yes, sometimes no—but couples therapy provides a safe space to explore whether and how to move forward.

Intimacy & Connection Issues

When you're more like roommates than partners. When physical intimacy has disappeared or feels obligatory. When you miss the closeness you used to have. We'll explore what's blocking intimacy—resentment, stress, past trauma, unspoken needs—and work on rebuilding emotional and physical connection.

Recurring Conflicts

The same fight over and over—about money, parenting, in-laws, household responsibilities. These surface conflicts usually point to deeper issues: feeling unvalued, unheard, or unsafe. We'll address both the immediate conflicts and the underlying dynamics.

Major Life Transitions

New baby, job changes, moves, illness, loss—major transitions stress relationships. You're both struggling, but instead of supporting each other, you're pulling apart. I'll help you navigate these transitions as a team and use challenges to strengthen your bond.

Deciding About the Relationship

Should you stay or go? Sometimes couples therapy helps you reconnect. Sometimes it helps you separate with clarity and compassion. Either way, I provide a safe space to explore your options and make the decision that's right for you.

My Approach to Couples Therapy

Creating Safety First

Before we can address problems, both partners need to feel safe—safe to be vulnerable, to express needs, to disagree. I create ground rules for respectful communication and ensure both voices are heard equally.

Understanding Patterns

Most relationship problems aren't about the specific issue—they're about the pattern. One person pursues, the other withdraws. One criticizes, the other defends. We'll identify your patterns and understand how they developed, often from past relationships or family dynamics.

Teaching Communication Skills

Most of us never learned how to communicate effectively in relationships. I'll teach you practical skills: active listening, expressing needs without blame, managing conflict, repairing after fights, and asking for what you need.

Addressing Individual Issues

Sometimes relationship problems stem from individual struggles—trauma, anxiety, depression, addiction. I'll help identify when individual therapy might also be helpful and coordinate care as needed.

Rebuilding Connection

Once we've addressed immediate conflicts, we focus on rebuilding emotional and physical intimacy. This includes creating rituals of connection, expressing appreciation, understanding each other's love languages, and prioritizing your relationship.

When to Seek Couples Therapy

Don't wait until your relationship is in crisis. Consider couples therapy if:

  • You're having the same arguments repeatedly without resolution
  • Communication has broken down or turned hostile
  • You feel more like roommates than partners
  • Physical or emotional intimacy has decreased significantly
  • There's been a betrayal (affair, lies, broken trust)
  • You're facing a major life transition together
  • One or both of you is considering separation
  • You feel lonely, unheard, or unappreciated in the relationship
  • Past trauma or family patterns are affecting your relationship
  • You want to strengthen your relationship before problems develop

The earlier you seek help, the easier it is to repair. Don't wait until resentment has built for years.

What to Expect in Couples Therapy

First Session: Assessment

I'll meet with both of you together to understand your relationship history, current challenges, and goals. I'll also assess for any safety concerns or individual issues that need attention.

Early Sessions: Building Foundation

We'll establish ground rules for communication, identify patterns, and start teaching skills. You'll likely have "homework"—practicing new communication techniques, scheduling date nights, or individual reflection.

Middle Phase: Deeper Work

As communication improves, we address deeper issues: past wounds, unmet needs, family-of-origin patterns, trauma. This phase can be challenging but is where real healing happens.

Later Sessions: Maintenance & Growth

Once you've made progress, sessions may become less frequent. We focus on maintaining gains, continuing to deepen connection, and preparing you to handle future challenges independently.

Timeline

Most couples see improvement within 8-12 sessions, though some issues (like rebuilding trust after an affair) may take 6-12 months or longer. We'll check in regularly about progress.

Common Questions

What if my partner won't come to therapy?

Individual therapy can still help. You can work on your own patterns, communication skills, and decide how to move forward. Sometimes when one partner starts therapy, the other becomes more willing to join.

Will you take sides?

No. My role is to be fair to both partners, understand both perspectives, and help you understand each other. I'm on the side of your relationship.

What if we decide to separate?

Sometimes couples therapy leads to conscious uncoupling rather than reconciliation. If separation is the healthiest choice, I'll support you in doing it with clarity and compassion, especially if children are involved.

Do you see couples who aren't married?

Absolutely. I work with all couples—dating, engaged, married, cohabitating, LGBTQ+, polyamorous. All relationships deserve support.
