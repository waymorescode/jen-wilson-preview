Eating Disorders & Body Image

Healing Your Relationship with Food and Your Body

Food Shouldn't Be a Battle

The constant mental math of calories. The rules about "good" and "bad" foods. The shame spiral after eating. The way you can't look in the mirror without criticizing every inch of your body. The exhaustion of thinking about food and weight all day, every day.

Maybe you restrict, binge, purge, or all of the above. Maybe you exercise compulsively or weigh yourself multiple times a day. Maybe you've tried every diet and nothing "works"—because the problem was never really about food or weight.

Eating disorders aren't about vanity or willpower. They're about pain, control, trauma, and trying to feel safe in a world that doesn't feel safe. And recovery is absolutely possible.

Eating Disorders I Treat

Anorexia Nervosa

The relentless drive to be smaller, the fear of gaining weight, the rules about eating that control your life. Anorexia tells you that you're never thin enough, that control equals safety. But underneath, it's often about trauma, perfectionism, or trying to disappear. Recovery means learning to nourish yourself—body and soul.

Bulimia Nervosa

The cycle of restricting, binging, and purging. The shame that keeps you trapped. The physical toll it takes. Bulimia often starts as a way to cope with overwhelming emotions, but it becomes its own prison. We'll work on breaking the cycle and healing the pain underneath.

Binge Eating Disorder

Eating past fullness, feeling out of control, then drowning in shame. Using food to numb emotions, then hating yourself for it. Binge eating isn't about lack of willpower—it's often about unprocessed trauma, anxiety, or using food as the only available coping mechanism. Recovery means finding other ways to cope and healing your relationship with food.

ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder)

Limited food preferences, fear of trying new foods, or anxiety around eating. ARFID isn't about weight or body image—it's about sensory issues, fear of choking, or past negative experiences with food. We'll work at your pace to expand your relationship with food.

Orthorexia

The obsession with "clean" or "healthy" eating that takes over your life. Rigid food rules, anxiety about ingredients, social isolation because you can't eat "normally." Orthorexia often masquerades as health, but it's really about control and anxiety. We'll help you find balance.

Body Dysmorphia

The inability to see your body accurately. Obsessing over perceived flaws. Avoiding mirrors or checking constantly. Body dysmorphia distorts your perception and steals your peace. We'll work on changing your relationship with your body and addressing the underlying anxiety.

Emotional Eating & Food Obsession

Using food to cope with stress, boredom, sadness, or anxiety. Thinking about food constantly. Feeling controlled by cravings. We'll explore what you're really hungry for and develop healthier coping strategies.

My Approach to Eating Disorder Recovery

Eating disorder recovery isn't about meal plans and food rules—it's about healing the pain underneath. My approach addresses the whole person, not just the symptoms.

Addressing Root Causes

Eating disorders often develop as a way to cope with trauma, anxiety, perfectionism, or feeling out of control. We'll explore what your eating disorder is trying to protect you from or help you control. This might include processing trauma, healing attachment wounds, or addressing family dynamics.

Challenging Diet Culture

Diet culture has taught us that our worth is tied to our weight, that we need to "earn" food, that our bodies are projects to be fixed. We'll work on unlearning these toxic messages and building a healthier relationship with food and your body.

Building Coping Skills

If food has been your primary way to cope with emotions, we'll develop new tools: distress tolerance skills, emotion regulation, mindfulness, self-compassion practices. You'll learn to sit with difficult feelings without turning to eating disorder behaviors.

Body Image Healing

Recovery isn't about loving your body every day—it's about making peace with it. We'll work on body neutrality, challenging negative thoughts, and separating your worth from your appearance.

Collaborative Care

Eating disorder recovery often requires a team. I work closely with dietitians, physicians, and psychiatrists to provide comprehensive care. If you don't already have a treatment team, I can provide referrals.

Do You Have an Eating Disorder?

Eating disorders can be hard to recognize, especially in yourself. Consider seeking help if:

  • You think about food, weight, or your body most of the day
  • You have rigid rules about what, when, or how much you can eat
  • You feel guilty, ashamed, or anxious after eating
  • You restrict food, skip meals, or avoid eating with others
  • You binge eat or feel out of control around food
  • You purge through vomiting, laxatives, or excessive exercise
  • You weigh yourself frequently or avoid the scale entirely
  • You avoid looking in mirrors or constantly check your appearance
  • Your eating behaviors interfere with your life, relationships, or health
  • You exercise compulsively or feel extreme anxiety if you can't exercise
  • You've lost your period (for women) or experienced other physical symptoms

You don't have to be underweight to have an eating disorder. Eating disorders affect people of all sizes, genders, and backgrounds.

What Recovery Looks Like

Recovery isn't linear, and it doesn't mean perfection. Here's what we'll work toward together:

Early Recovery: Safety & Stabilization

If you're medically unstable, we'll coordinate with your doctor and possibly a dietitian. We'll work on reducing harmful behaviors, building coping skills, and creating safety. This phase can be challenging, but you won't do it alone.

Middle Recovery: Addressing Root Causes

As behaviors stabilize, we dig deeper. What is your eating disorder protecting you from? What pain are you trying to control or numb? We'll process trauma, challenge perfectionism, improve relationships, and build a life worth recovering for.

Late Recovery: Building Your Life

Recovery isn't just about stopping eating disorder behaviors—it's about creating a life where you don't need them. We'll work on relationships, career, hobbies, self-compassion, and finding meaning beyond your eating disorder.

Timeline

Eating disorder recovery takes time—often 1-2 years or more. But you'll notice improvements along the way: less anxiety around food, more energy, better relationships, moments of peace with your body. Progress isn't always linear, and that's okay.

You Deserve to Be Free

Free from food rules, body shame, and the exhausting mental battle. Recovery is possible, and you don't have to do it alone.
